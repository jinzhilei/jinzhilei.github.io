Research

Our research sits at the interface of applied mathematics, systems biology, and cancer biology.

Research Overview

We develop mathematical and computational models to understand the quantitative principles underlying biological systems, with a particular focus on cancer evolution and stem cell biology. Our work combines deterministic dynamical systems, stochastic processes, and multi-scale modelling to bridge molecular mechanisms and population-level behaviour.

Research Directions

1. Cancer Evolution Dynamics and Mathematical Oncology

Cancer is a complex, multi-scale dynamical process. We develop mathematical frameworks to study how tumour cell populations evolve under selection pressure, acquire drug resistance, and relapse after treatment. Key questions include:

  • How do clonal heterogeneity and epigenetic plasticity shape treatment outcomes?
  • What mathematical signatures predict treatment-free remission in chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML)?
  • How can adaptive therapy be optimally designed for metastatic castrate-resistant prostate cancer?

Representative work:

  • Lai, X., Jiao, X., Zhang, H., Lei, J.* (2024). Computational modeling reveals key factors driving treatment-free remission in chronic myeloid leukemic patients. npj Systems Biology and Applications, 10:45.
  • Wang, D., Lei, J.* (2025). Optimal adaptive therapeutic schedule for metastatic castrate-resistant prostate cancer. [in submission]

2. Stem Cell Proliferation and Multi-scale Modelling

We construct multi-scale stochastic and deterministic models of stem cell behaviour in haematopoietic and epithelial tissues. A central theme is understanding how cross-talk between genetic circuits and epigenetic states governs the balance between self-renewal and differentiation.

Representative work:

  • Lei, J., Levin, S.A., Nie, Q. (2014). Mathematical model of adult stem cell regeneration with cross-talk between genetic and epigenetic regulation. PNAS, 111:E880–E887.
  • Jiang, H., Liu, J.X., Song, Y., Lei, J.* (2024). Quantitative modeling of stemness in single-cell RNA sequencing data. Journal of Theoretical Biology, 31(1):1–17.

3. Epigenetic Cell-Type Transitions

Epigenetic modifications—such as DNA methylation and histone modifications—act as a dynamical layer of gene regulation that mediates cell-fate decisions. We develop computational frameworks to model cell-type transitions as trajectories in an epigenetic landscape (Waddington landscape), incorporating heterogeneity, plasticity, and entropy.

Representative work:

  • Huang, R., Situ, Q., Lei, J.* (2024). Dynamics of cell-type transition mediated by epigenetic modifications. Journal of Theoretical Biology, 577:111664.
  • Lei, J. (2020). Evolutionary dynamics of cancer: From epigenetic regulation to cell population dynamics. Science China Mathematics, 63(3):411–424.

4. Haematopoietic System Dynamics and Blood Disorders

We study the dynamics of blood cell production under normal and pathological conditions. Our models address cyclic haematopoiesis, periodic chronic myelogenous leukaemia, and the design of optimal control strategies for treatment.

Representative work:

  • Lei, J., Mackey, M.C. (2011). Deterministic Brownian motion generated from differential delay equations. Physical Review E, 84:041105.

5. Gene Regulatory Networks and Developmental Biology

Using mathematical modelling and dynamical systems analysis, we investigate how gene networks encode developmental programmes—from Drosophila imaginal disc patterning to organoid morphogenesis.

Methods We Use

Our toolkit spans a wide range of mathematical and computational methods:

Method Application
Ordinary & delay differential equations Deterministic population dynamics
Stochastic differential equations Cell-level noise and variability
Markov chain / master equations Discrete stochastic gene expression
Optimal control theory Adaptive therapy design
Single-cell data analysis Stemness scoring, trajectory inference
Agent-based modelling Tumour growth and heterogeneity
